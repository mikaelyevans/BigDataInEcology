Introduction

Deforestation is a practice that has destroyed forests across the globe for centuries. Rainforests are a prime target for deforestation due to the rare resources they offer, which cannot be found elsewhere. Forest restoration is the practice of planting trees to revive struggling forests into the functional ecosystems they once were. One specific benefit of rainforest restoration is the potential for lessening effects of climate change, since rainforests are carbon sinks and have a high potential for converting CO2 to H20 (Localetti et al.). Some other benefits of forest restoration include maintaining nutrient cycling in those ecosystems (Koch et al.) and restoring habitats for native species (Edwards et al.). Most forest restoration projects focus on the larger canopy trees, and forest understory is often forgotten due to limitations in time and budgets. This canopy-focused reforestation can leave restored forests vastly different from remnant forests due to differences in understory growth. In the Hakalau Forest National Wildlife Reserve, the U.S. Forest Service has been restoring the native Acacia Koa Tree population. This tree is a nitrogen fixing tree and scientists predict that as this tree takes root again, litterfall and nitrogen availability will increase (Hughes et al.), soil characteristics will change (D’Antonio et al.), and competition for understory plants will change, shifting overall ecosystem dynamics. Scientists from the U.S.F.S. created three types of experimental plots: “OP” Acacia Koa tree without restored understory, “AK” Acacia Koa tree with restored woody understory, and “RO” Metrosideros Polymorpha tree with naturally recruited native understory.

This experimental design prompted me to ask three research questions:
1) How does understory basal area differ across experimental plots?
2) How does nitrogen abundance differ across experimental plots?
3) How does microbial biomass differ across experimental plots?

I hypothesize that:
1) Plots with native M. Polymorpha will have larger understory basal area than other experimental plots because the native recruited understory of RO plots has had more time to grow and develop.
2) Nitrogen will be more abundant in plots with A. Koa because it can fix N from the atmosphere and transport it into the soil, theoretically increasing soil N.
3) Microbial biomass in plots with an understory will be higher than plots without due to the understory inputting more carbon into the soil through organic matter.

Discussion

After making plots and performing linear regressions to analyze the hypotheses, I confirm that:
1) My hypothesis was confirmed, plots with native M. Polymorpha have statistically greater understory mass than other experimental plots.
2) My hypothesis was confirmed, nitrogen abundance differs across experimental plots. Compared to the open A. koa plots, litter N increased in restored understory plots with A. Koa and decreased in native recruited understory plots. There was no significant difference in soil N across plots, this is possibly due to a time lag that is not accounted for in the absorption of litter N into the soil.
3) My hypothesis was rejected, there is not a significant increase in microbial biomass in plots with an understory compared to plots without.

These findings highlight the need to prioritize the restoration of forest understories alongside canopy restoration, given the significant differences between restored and remnant forests. Rehabilitating forest understories will enhance habitat creation, improve soil health, support native plant recruitment, and increase carbon sequestration, ultimately contributing to healthier ecosystems.

Some limitations to this experiment include the limitations in time frame and sample size. If this experiment were able to monitor soil N for a longer period of time, it is possible that we could see the effects from the litter N on the soil. Along with this, it would be beneficial to run the experiment on more plots to have more powerful results. Finally, in the tropical rainforest of Hawaii, there are great differences in elevation across the ecosystem which changes understory makeup. I would be intrigued to look into how this experiment would differ across elevation gradients in the mountains. Some other ideas that expand on ideas from this project include experimenting with non-native understories. Since non-native species are abundant, it is important to know how they are affecting the soil and understory. It would also interest me to look into plant-animal interactions across the restored vs. recruited understories. Since these understories house different species, they may provide different ecosystem services and support different communities. It is very interesting to be able to look beyond the canopy and understory to see how these species affect the ecosystem as a whole.
